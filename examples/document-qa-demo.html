Quantum Computing Research Notes Quantum computers use quantum bits (qubits) instead of classical bits. While classical bits can only be in states 0 or 1, qubits can exist in a superposition of both states simultaneously. This property, combined with quantum entanglement, gives quantum computers their unique computational advantages. Current State of Technology: - IBM, Google, and others have built quantum computers with 50-100 qubits - These systems require extreme cooling (near absolute zero) - Error rates remain a significant challenge - Quantum supremacy was claimed by Google in 2019 Potential Applications: 1. Cryptography: Breaking current encryption methods, creating quantum-secure communications 2. Drug Discovery: Simulating molecular interactions at quantum level 3. Optimization: Solving complex logistics and scheduling problems 4. Machine Learning: Faster training of certain AI models Challenges: - Quantum decoherence (qubits losing their quantum state) - High error rates requiring error correction - Scalability issues as qubit count increases - Extremely expensive infrastructure requirements Timeline: Experts predict practical quantum advantage for specific applications within 5-10 years, though general-purpose quantum computing may take decades.
